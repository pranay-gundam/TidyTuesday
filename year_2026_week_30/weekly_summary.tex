Welcome back to another thrilling installment of Two Random Time Series Walk Into a Regression, the segment where we let a computer play matchmaker between economic data that has never met and probably never should. This week the algorithm, in its infinite wisdom, decided to pair the Federal Reserve's holdings of medium-term Treasury securities with -- and I cannot stress how excited you should be about this -- the Consumer Price Index for Services in the South, Size Class D, a series so beloved that it has a popularity rating of 1 and was discontinued back in 2017. Truly, the Fed's bond portfolio has found its soulmate in a defunct regional price index. Somebody get these two a couples' registry.

And the raw regression came out swinging! An R-squared of 0.77, a p-value with more zeros after the decimal than I have the patience to count, the whole nine yards. If you stopped reading right here, you might march off convinced that the amount of Treasuries the Fed holds is somehow psychically linked to what Southerners were paying for services during the Carter-through-Obama era. It's a beautiful, statistically significant love story. It is also, and I say this with great affection, complete nonsense -- because both of these series spend their entire lives politely drifting upward over time, and if you line up any two things that both go up, congratulations, you've discovered a "relationship." So has everyone who has ever noticed that both their age and the sea level increase.

This is exactly why we bother with the detrended version, where we rudely strip the shared upward march out of both series and see what's left. And what's left is... a considerable comedown. The R-squared face-plants from a swaggering 0.77 down to a much more sheepish 0.37, and the coefficient even flips its sign from positive to negative, which is data's way of saying "actually, never mind, forget I said anything." To be fair to our star-crossed pair, the detrended relationship is technically still significant, so this isn't a total mirage evaporating in the desert heat -- but the drama has clearly been downgraded from "epic romance" to "two strangers who once made brief, confusing eye contact."

So what have we learned? Roughly nothing, and that's the whole point. These are randomly paired series with no causal story, no theoretical grounding, and absolutely no business being in the same spreadsheet -- the strong raw number was mostly two trends holding hands, and even the surviving detrended blip is exactly the kind of coincidence you'd expect to trip over when you keep flinging random data at each other week after week. Spurious correlation isn't the bug here, it's the entire premise. Tune in next week, when the machine will pair something equally unrelated and I will once again pretend to be shocked.
