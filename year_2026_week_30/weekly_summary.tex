Welcome back to the only recurring publication where a computer flings two unrelated economic datasets at each other and we all pretend to be surprised by the results. This week, our tireless robot pulled just two pairings, which is honestly a merciful workload for a project whose entire premise is manufacturing false hope. Let's dig in and feel some emotions about numbers that mean nothing.

Monday, July 21, gave us a truly inspired matchup: the Federal Reserve's holdings of medium-term Treasury securities versus a Consumer Price Index for services in the American South that has been discontinued since 2017 and boasts a FRED popularity rating of one. One. That is the loneliest number a data series can have, and we honored it by regressing it against the Fed's balance sheet. The raw regression came back with an R-squared of 0.77 and a p-value with more zeros after the decimal than I care to count, which in a less enlightened newsletter would trigger a champagne cork. But then we detrended it, stripped out the shared "everything drifts upward over decades" effect, and watched the R-squared deflate to 0.37 while the relationship politely flipped sign. Translation: the raw "connection" was mostly two lines wandering the same general direction like strangers who happen to be walking to the same bus stop. A textbook trend mirage, and exactly the kind of thing this project exists to point at and laugh.

Tuesday, July 22, however, is where I need you to sit down. We paired "Withdrawals from Income of Quasi-Corporations" -- a phrase I refuse to believe is real -- against the current-cost depreciation of nonresidential structures. The raw regression posted an R-squared of 0.98, which is the kind of number that makes you want to call your mother. And here's the genuinely annoying part: after detrending, it held. R-squared of 0.87, p-value still buried in scientific notation, coefficient barely budging from 0.42 to 0.40. That is not a mirage collapsing in the desert; that is a relationship standing there smugly, arms crossed, in both the raw and detrended versions. When two quirky, unpopular series both survive the trend-removal gauntlet, it's at least a little tempting to imagine a real thread -- both are dollar-denominated measures of business and capital activity, so maybe they genuinely rise and fall with the same underlying economic tides. Or maybe not.

Because -- and I cannot stress this enough -- these pairings are chosen at random by a program with no theory, no hypothesis, and no dignity. Nothing here is a causal claim, a forecast, or a reason to move money. Spurious correlation isn't the occasional bug in this operation; it's the entire product line, lovingly hand-picked from FRED's forgotten basement. Tuesday's result is "interesting" only in the sense that it survived a slightly harder test than Monday's, and even that just means we should be marginally less embarrassed to mention it at a party. See you next week, when the robot will almost certainly correlate cheese production with bond yields and we'll all act astonished again.
